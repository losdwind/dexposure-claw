\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

We evaluate \textsc{DeXposure-Claw} on the \textsc{DeXposure-Bench} suite (\S\ref{sec:bench}), grouping the six benchmarks under two natural axes: (i)~does the forecast--measure pipeline produce useful, calibrated, and robust risk signals (Task~I, covering b1, b2, b3, b6), and (ii)~do the resulting decision tickets improve the recall--explanation operating point while exposing their intervention cost (Task~II, covering b4, b5)? The result narrative follows the structure of the benchmark itself: Fig.~\ref{fig:eval_framework} establishes the six-axis evaluation stage, Table~\ref{tab:task1_summary} and Fig.~\ref{fig:task1_deployable_signal} audit where the FM helps and where persistence remains stronger, Table~\ref{tab:task2_table3} and Fig.~\ref{fig:task2_method_comparison} isolate the layer-wise contribution and cost of FM, LLM, and safety gate, and the timeline/ablation tables close with reliability evidence. Unless explicitly marked as a historical event study, results are reported on the frozen 2025 test split of Table~\ref{tab:bench-split}; the ground truth, dataset split, and reference methods used throughout are defined in \S\ref{sec:bench}.

\subsection{Task I: Network Risk Monitoring}

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}
\label{sssec:impl_task1}

\paragraph{Data and temporal granularity}
We use the \textsc{DeXposure} dataset~\cite{wu2025dexposuredatasetbenchmarksinterprotocol}, comprising weekly directed weighted exposure-graph snapshots sourced from the DeFiLlama API. The dataset spans 2020-03 to 2025-08 (283~weeks), with 50--500 protocol nodes and 100--2{,}000 exposure edges per snapshot, totalling over 43.7M data entries. Node features include log-TVL, number of token types, maximum token share, Shannon entropy of token distribution, and protocol category (one-hot, 15~classes). Edge weights are log-scaled USD exposure values. We evaluate multi-horizon forecasting on $\mathcal{H}=\{1,4,8,12\}$ weeks.

We enforce a strict no-leakage temporal split:

\paragraph{Forecast interface and predicted-graph construction}
For each epoch $t$ and horizon $h\in\mathcal{H}$, we query the backbone model to obtain a predictive distribution $\mathcal{P}_{t,h}$. We construct an expected-weight predicted graph $\widehat{G}_{t+h}$ by thresholding edge existence probabilities at $\pi_{\min}$ and setting $\widehat{w}_{pq} := \Pr(e_{pq}=1)\cdot \mathbb{E}[w_{pq}\mid e_{pq}=1]$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:expected_graph}). We set $\pi_{\min}=0.2$ on the validation split. For uncertainty quantification, we draw $M=50$ Monte Carlo samples via Bernoulli edge sampling and Gaussian weight perturbation.

\paragraph{Monitoring metrics and alert triggers}
At each horizon, we compute five monitoring functionals $\mathbf{N}_{t,h}=\Phi(\widehat{G}_{t+h},X_t)$ (Table~\ref{tab:metrics}): systemic importance (N1), HHI concentration (N2), network density (N3), PageRank Gini (N4), and degree Gini (N5). Baseline bands $(\mu_t^{(j)}, \sigma_t^{(j)})$ are computed via a rolling window of $L=26$ weeks. An alert fires when a metric exceeds $z=2$ standard deviations from baseline (Eq.~\eqref{eq:alert_trigger}). Alert confidence follows Eq.~\eqref{eq:alert_confidence}.

\paragraph{Stress scenarios}
We evaluate five standardised scenarios $\mathcal{S}_0$ (Table~\ref{tab:scenarios}): single protocol failure (S1, 100\% shock on top node), bridge cluster failure (S2, 100\% on bridges), stablecoin de-peg (S3, 50\% on stablecoins), sector-wide lending shock (S4, 30\%), and correlated stress (S5, 20\% on top-10 nodes). CVaR is computed as the mean of the worst $\lambda=0.5$ fraction of MC sample losses (Eq.~\eqref{eq:cvar}).

\paragraph{Attribution}
For each triggered alert, we rank contributing edges by marginal contribution: $\mathrm{Contrib}(e) := \Phi^{(j)}(\widehat{G}_{t+h}) - \Phi^{(j)}(\widehat{G}_{t+h}\setminus e)$, retaining the top $K=10$.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol}
Within the six-axis framework of Fig.~\ref{fig:eval_framework}, Task~I evaluates the forecast--measure pipeline along three axes:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt]
    \item \emph{Risk-metric forecasting (b1_forecast):} predicted vs.\ realised metrics using MAE and Spearman rank correlation, for each $h\in\{1,4,8,12\}$.
    \item \emph{Early-warning quality (b2_warning):} lead time to stress events, precision/recall under alert budgets $\{5,10,20\}$, alert stability (flip rate), and F1-warning.
    \item \emph{Uncertainty calibration (b3_calibration):} expected calibration error (ECE), prediction interval coverage (target 90\%), PI width, and CRPS. We apply split conformal prediction on the validation set to ensure asymptotically valid coverage.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Reproducibility}
All hyperparameters ($\pi_{\min}$, $L$, $z$, $K$, $M$, $\tau_{\mathrm{data}}$) are selected on the validation split and fixed for test evaluation. Random seeds are fixed for MC sampling and LLM consistency checks. The pipeline is fail-closed on predictors: an unavailable FM or EvolveGCN checkpoint aborts the corresponding run rather than silently falling back to persistence. For \texttt{b2\_warning} we report the shared weighted-degree heuristic (\texttt{h1\_weighted\_degree}), since b2's monitoring layer sits upstream of the predictor choice and is therefore not a method-specific comparison.

\subsubsection{Empirical Results}
Table~\ref{tab:task1_summary} reports the Task~I evidence ledger used in the main text; the full all-horizon \texttt{b1\_forecast} matrix is provided as Table~\ref{tab:b1_all_horizons} in Appendix~\ref{app:b1_full}. The point of the table is not to claim that the FM wins every forecasting metric. Instead, it separates three types of evidence: 2025 predictor-specific evidence (b1, b3, b6), a historical monitor event study (b2), and the strong persistence baseline that any deployable monitor must acknowledge.

\paragraph{Persistence is a deceptively strong point-metric baseline} On PageRank MAE, Gini MAE, and rank correlation, persistence matches or marginally beats the FM at every horizon (rank correlation $0.570$ vs $0.558$ at $h{=}4$; PR MAE $3.4$ vs $4.5$ in $10^{-5}$ units). Weekly DeFi exposure structure is heavily auto-correlated, so any predictor that returns the most recent graph captures the dominant source of variance ``for free''---the same phenomenon that makes seasonal-naive a strong baseline in classical forecasting. A new model must clear this bar before its other properties matter.

\paragraph{FM gains are concentrated in trend and robustness, not in absolute error} The FM produces a non-trivial trend signal (trend consistency $0.525$--$0.632$ across horizons) while persistence is structurally pinned at $0$. Under data degradation (\texttt{b6\_robustness}, mean relative degradation $0.113$ vs $0.148$ for persistence), the FM drops $24\%$ less on average. These results support a narrower claim than pointwise superiority: the FM contributes direction, uncertainty, and robustness features that persistence does not provide, while persistence remains the better static point-metric baseline.

\paragraph{Calibration is strong; learned GNN transfer remains difficult} The FM achieves PI coverage $0.913$ against a $0.90$ target with ECE $0.013$ on \texttt{b3\_calibration}, both within the expected slack of split-conformal calibration. Persistence has no native uncertainty estimate and is omitted from this row. EvolveGCN is generally weaker than persistence and FM variants on the downstream metrics in the appendices, which we attribute to data efficiency: $222$ weekly training snapshots fall below the regime where it learns a useful structural prior, while the FM benefits from large-scale pre-training transfer.

\subsection{Task II: Decision-Making}

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}
\label{sssec:impl_task2}

\paragraph{Decision timeline and inputs}
Task~II operates at the same decision epochs as Task~I. At each epoch, the decision agent receives: (i)~the current state $(G_t,X_t)$, (ii)~multi-horizon forecasts $\{\widehat{G}_{t+h}\}_{h\in\mathcal{H}}$ and optional MC samples, (iii)~monitoring alerts $\mathcal{A}_t$ with evidence and confidence scores, and (iv)~the scenario summary $\mathcal{R}_t$.

\paragraph{Action space and playbook}
We use a four-level conservative playbook (Table~\ref{tab:playbook}): \texttt{Monitor} (severity weight 0.25, always feasible), \texttt{Investigate} (0.50, always feasible), \texttt{Recommend-Reduce} (0.75, requires $\mathrm{SAFE\_MODE}=0$ and $\ge 2$ alerts), and \texttt{Contingency} (1.00, requires $\mathrm{SAFE\_MODE}=0$ and $\ge 3$ alerts). Intervention-type actions additionally require mean alert confidence $\bar{C}_t \ge \tau_{\mathrm{conf}}=0.6$.

\paragraph{Ticket scoring}
Tickets are ranked by $\mathrm{Score}_t(u) = w_u \cdot \bar{C}_t \cdot \mathrm{Imp}_t$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:ticket_score}), where $w_u$ is the playbook severity weight, $\bar{C}_t$ is mean alert confidence, and $\mathrm{Imp}_t$ is the mean CVaR across all scenarios (defaulting to~1.0 when no scenarios are available). We output the top-$N=10$ tickets.

\paragraph{Target selection}
For intervention tickets, targets are selected from the union of top-$K$ attributed nodes across triggering alerts and worst-case scenarios, ranked by frequency-weighted vote.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol}
We evaluate Task~II using both offline and scenario-based criteria:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt]
    \item \emph{Offline (b5_decision):} ticket precision (fraction followed by real stress), target stability (Jaccard between adjacent epochs), audit completeness (evidence bundle coverage), suppression rate (safe-mode activation), and false intervention rate.
    \item \emph{Scenario-based (b4_stress):} stress-test contagion prediction accuracy---loss MAE, distressed count MAE, propagation depth MAE, and target overlap@$K$ between predicted and actual networks under scenarios S1--S5.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Reproducibility}
All decision hyperparameters ($K$, $N$, $\tau_{\mathrm{data}}$, $\tau_{\mathrm{conf}}$, $\lambda$) are tuned on the validation split and fixed for testing. MC components use fixed seeds; LLM consistency is measured through repeated deterministic calls and reported in Table~\ref{tab:task2_table3}.

The table should be read as a multi-objective comparison rather than a single leaderboard: \texttt{m1\_persistence\_rules} is the highest-precision conservative baseline, \texttt{m5\_fm\_rules} gives the strongest rule-based F1 balance, and the LLM variants trade false-intervention cost for higher recall and explanation quality.

\input{sections/T2-MethodComparison.tex}

\paragraph{Persistence+Rules wins on single-metric precision} \texttt{m1\_persistence\_rules} achieves the highest ticket precision ($0.720$) and, because its false intervention rate is $0$, also the highest FIR-penalised cost-adjusted score ($0.720$). This is consistent with conservative rule-engine behaviour: it raises few tickets and most are correct. It is the right baseline for any system that wants to claim improvement on decision quality.

\paragraph{FM+LLM triples recall but at a real precision cost} \texttt{m6\_fm\_llm} raises recall from $0.004$ (persistence) to $0.012$---a $3\times$ improvement on the regulator-aligned recall axis---at the cost of precision ($0.570$ vs $0.720$) and a substantial false intervention rate (FIR $0.448$). The safety gate (\texttt{m7\_fm\_llm\_gated}) partially mitigates this and raises the LLM-as-judge score from $2.48$ to $2.69$/$5$, but does not fully recover precision. Both LLM variants reach grounding $1.000$, so the failure mode is not hallucination---it is over-reaching to high-severity actions when monitor evidence is weak.

\paragraph{Layer-wise decomposition} Holding the decision policy fixed at LLM and adding the FM signal lifts ticket F1 by $32\%$ ($0.0183 \to 0.0241$); holding the predictor fixed at FM and swapping rules for LLM lifts F1 by another $27\%$ ($0.0189 \to 0.0241$). The two layers therefore make additive, non-substitutable contributions to F1. Adding the safety gate trades $3\%$ F1 for $+0.21$ judge score, which is the operating point a deployment operator should select when explanation quality is the binding constraint.

\paragraph{Early-warning timeline analysis}
To determine whether the weighted-degree monitoring alerts (\texttt{h1\_weighted\_degree}) constitute genuine \emph{early warnings} or merely post-hoc damage reports, we conduct a per-week historical alert timeline analysis around the Terra/Luna (2022-05-09), FTX (2022-11-07), and SVB/USDC (2023-03-10) events. Table~\ref{tab:alert_timeline} summarises the results.

\input{sections/T3-AlertTimeline.tex}

The data supports pre-event lead time for this monitor: median lead times are $4$--$5$ weeks across all three crises, with SVB/USDC reaching precision $1.000$ at every alert budget. This should be read as historical monitor evidence, not as an FM-specific test-split result. F1 is small in absolute terms because the regulator-aligned ground-truth pool $\mathcal{S}_t^h$ contains $\sim 283$ protocols per week, so a budget of $5$--$20$ alerts covers only a thin slice of the recall surface.

\paragraph{Stress-test fidelity (\texttt{b4\_stress})} Per-scenario stress-test outputs are reported in Table~\ref{tab:b4_detail} (Appendix~\ref{app:b4_detail}). As with \texttt{b1\_forecast}, persistence is a strong baseline because each scenario applies a structural shock to the current graph and the realised loss vector therefore correlates strongly with the persistence prediction. The FM variants roughly match persistence on Loss MAE and Overlap@$10$ and sometimes marginally improve loss MAE, but the differences are small; this table is best read as evidence that the scenario engine remains competitive with persistence rather than as a sweeping FM win. EvolveGCN is generally weaker on target overlap and distressed-count fidelity, mirroring its weakness on \texttt{b1\_forecast}.

\subsection{Ablation Study}

We separate clean-data load-bearing components from stress-reserve mechanisms through ablation experiments under standard and targeted stress conditions (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}).

\input{sections/T4-Ablation.tex}

\paragraph{Standard conditions (Panel~A)} Under clean 2025 test data, the confidence gate (A2) is load-bearing: removing it increases the false intervention rate from $0.000$ to $0.429$. The scenario engine (A3) is also load-bearing for target extraction: skipping it reduces ticket precision from $0.600$ to $0.000$. Data-health gating (A1) and multi-horizon diversity (A6) show no measurable impact in this clean setting because data quality is high and $h=4$ dominates alert generation.

\paragraph{Stress conditions (Panels~B--C)} To test whether A1 and A6 are unnecessary or merely dormant on clean data, we evaluate them under targeted stress conditions. For A1, we apply severe feature/edge degradation regimes (80--98\% masking) and isolate the data-health gate from the confidence gate by setting $\tau_{\mathrm{conf}}=0$. Under these conditions, disabling the data-health gate increases the false intervention rate to $0.27$--$0.60$ (Panel~B), while triggered safe-mode suppresses interventions back to $0.000$, showing that A1 functions as a \emph{safety reserve under data degradation}. For A6, we compare single-horizon ($h=4$) against multi-horizon ($h\in\{1,4,8,12\}$) monitoring across the Terra/Luna, FTX, and SVB/USDC crisis windows: multi-horizon generates roughly $4\times$ more alerts (73--98 vs.\ 19--23) while maintaining the same precision range ($0.533$--$0.624$) and zero false interventions (Panel~C). Thus A6 broadens alert coverage in crisis windows, but Table~\ref{tab:ablation} does not show a clean-data precision gain. The ablation evidence therefore supports a two-part interpretation: A2 and A3 carry the clean deployment path, while A1 and A6 are reserve mechanisms for degraded-data and crisis-window operation.
