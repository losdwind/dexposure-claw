\begin{abstract}
Decentralized finance exposes supervisors to fast-moving, networked credit risks in which shocks can propagate across protocols before human operators can inspect raw on-chain data. General-purpose LLM agents fit this setting poorly: they over-read weak or stale evidence and recommend high-stakes interventions, and existing evaluations offer no regulator-aligned way to measure the resulting false alarms. We introduce \sysname{}, a forecast-grounded agentic supervision system that routes LLM decisions through structured evidence rather than raw transactions: \fmname{}, a graph time-series foundation model, forecasts future exposure networks; deterministic monitors and stress scenarios convert these into alerts and scenario evidence; and data-health and confidence gates fire before \sysname{} emits auditable supervisory tickets with rationales. We pair it with \benchname{}, a six-axis harness whose decision axis scores tickets against a regulator-aligned absolute-loss ground truth and a false-intervention rate, released with eight reference implementations and ticket-level audit logs. On five years of weekly DeFi exposure graphs, routing through forecasted evidence raises ticket F1 over a conservative rules baseline from 0.0076 to 0.0233, yet the Opus~4.7 decision model still over-reads it, misfiring on roughly 44\% of interventions (false-intervention rate 0.437); a cheaper Sonnet~4.6 model on the same evidence raises F1 to 0.0288, drives measured false interventions to zero, and directionally improves explanation quality. We therefore cast \sysname{} as a recall--explanation Pareto option for human-in-the-loop DeFi supervision whose safe operating point depends on decision-model calibration, not a replacement for conservative rules.
\end{abstract}
